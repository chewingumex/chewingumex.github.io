CashAbroad Customer Churn Model




Intro

Senior Management at CashAbroad are interested in the loss (churn) of their existing customer base, and whether a customer is likely to churn or not can be predicted.

This report gives an overview of the churning distributions of CashAbroad’s customer base as well as the results of a model which predicts with ~ 95% accuracy (i.e. it predicts 95% of the times correctly) whether a customer churns or not. It is assumed that the value of 1 in the churn variable means the customer churned, that is, they are currently no longer customers of CashAbroad.

Certain inconsistencies in the data were addressed prior to visualisation and modeling. Data cleaning was performed filtering observations with simple errors as well as other arguably more serious data quality issues (i.e. some users were under 18 which presents ethical sometimes legal issues). For more details on data cleaning, please refer to the code provided. Below a summary of the data set.

These visualisations offer a good overview of the churn distributions of CashAbroad’s customer base. The majority of CashAbroad’s customers are male and in both genders we observe a healthy customer retention rate. Gender “unknown” was deliberately kept given the large volume of customers in this segment.

Churning by country gives a good overview of CashAbroad’s operations across the markets revised in this study and shows a relatively similar churn rate across the English speaking markets. Germany deserves attention since more customers have churned than not. In other words, the German customer base is decreasing. This is definitely an opportunity for CashAbroad to revise its customer retention efforts there and performing a deeper analysis of the situation is recommended. The UK shows the highest retention rates of all markets and perhaps some successful strategies adopted there could be adapted on the German market.

In terms of age, we observe that the majority of customers who continue with CashAbroad are between 35 and 55 years old whereas the number of churning customers peaks twice: at the ages of 30 and 60. It is interesting to reflect on the reasons for customers of these ages in particular to decide to continue receiving CashAbroad services or not. Furthermore, it becomes clear that a linear model won’t do to accurately represent the data given the bimodality of these crucial distributions.

Contact Propensity shows relatively little differentiation for both groups showing the highest number of users at the low end of the spectrum. However, the distribution of churning customers is even more starkly skewed to the right than its counterpart.

In terms of joining date, we observe a large number of customers leaving just before December 2020, perhaps engaging in a “one off” type of transaction before Christmas. This churn distribution could also be explained by the beginning of the latest lock-down in the UK and in most places. Remaining customers are, perhaps as expected, the most recent ones.

  drop_na(churn_flag)

  filter(is.na(churn_flag)) %>%

write.csv(trainData,"trainData.csv", quote = F, row.names = F)

Some pre-processing was done on the data to prepare it for modeling. From the joining date variable, all the information possible was extracted (year, month, quarter, week, day of the month, day of the week and day of the quarter). These new variables as well as gender and country were one-hot encoded given they are categorical resulting in 163 new variables. The observations where the churn status of the user is unknown (i.e. “NA”) were kept aside as test data (n = 5,621). The rest (n= 56,199) were used for modeling; two thirds of the observations were used to train the model and a third to validate it.

A neural network was built using the Tensorflow/keras api. The network comprises two hidden layers with 128 units each and a third layer with a single unit which outputs the probability of churn. The model trained for ~ 70 epochs given the early stopping parameter which stops it from training once binary cross-entropy didn’t reduce any further on the validation set. A reduction of the learning rate was scheduled on plateauing to reach a global or an acceptably low local minima. Regularisation net (i.e. L1 and L2) was implemented on the bias and kernel of both layers in order to produce a simple enough model which wouldn’t overfit the training data and would perform as well on new data.

Below two visualisations: binary cross-entropy (loss) falling and the area under the ROC curve increasing as the model trained on. In short, as the model continued to train the error diminished and the variance explained increased, both being objectives of the modeling process. In addition, a snippet of the prediction table of the validation set and classification accuracy, F1 score and the confusion matrix showing double as many users predicted erroneously as churned (FP) than users predicted erroneously as continuing customers (FN). Although really good results overall, it should be taken into account that the errors of the model are twice as likely to mistake an active user for a churned one than viceversa. Although errors are bound to occur only ~ 5% of the times, which is rather satisfactory, this particular error tendency should be kept in mind when putting the model in production or actioning its insights manually.


# import libraries
import tensorflow as tf
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
from sklearn.model_selection import train_test_split
from sklearn.metrics import confusion_matrix
from sklearn.metrics import f1_score
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# read in data and subset into training, validation and testing sets
# reshaping numpy arrays to fit tensorflow requirements

allData = pd.read_csv("trainData.csv").drop("customer_id", axis = 1)
xtest = pd.read_csv("testData.csv")
xtest_id = xtest.loc[:,"customer_id"]
xtest = xtest.drop("customer_id", axis = 1)
Xall, Yall = allData.drop("churn_flag", axis =1), allData.loc[:,"churn_flag"]
xtrain, xvalid, ytrain, yvalid = train_test_split(Xall, Yall, test_size=0.33, random_state=42)
xtrain, xvalid, ytrain, yvalid, xtest = xtrain.to_numpy(), xvalid.to_numpy(), ytrain.to_numpy(), yvalid.to_numpy(), xtest.to_numpy()
xtrain, xvalid, xtest = np.reshape(xtrain, (xtrain.shape[0],1,xtrain.shape[1])), np.reshape(xvalid, (xvalid.shape[0],1,xvalid.shape[1])), np.reshape(xtest, (xtest.shape[0],1,xtest.shape[1]))

# define and compile model
tf.keras.backend.clear_session()
regs = tf.keras.regularizers.l1_l2(l1=0.0001,l2=0.0001)
model = tf.keras.Sequential([
        tf.keras.layers.Dense(128, activation = 'relu', input_shape=(None, xtrain.shape[2]),kernel_regularizer=regs, bias_regularizer=regs),
        tf.keras.layers.Dense(128, activation = 'relu', kernel_regularizer=regs, bias_regularizer=regs),
        tf.keras.layers.Dense(1, activation = 'sigmoid')])

my_callbacks = [tf.keras.callbacks.ReduceLROnPlateau(monitor='val_loss', patience=5, verbose=1), 
                tf.keras.callbacks.EarlyStopping(monitor="val_loss", patience=10,verbose=1, restore_best_weights=True)]
    
model.compile(loss='binary_crossentropy', optimizer="Adam", metrics=["AUC"])

# fit model
history = model.fit(x=xtrain, 
          y=ytrain,
          validation_data=(xvalid, yvalid),
          epochs=1000, 
          verbose=1, 
          batch_size=100, 
          callbacks=[my_callbacks])
# model assessment

# plots
AUC = history.history['auc']
val_AUC = history.history['val_auc']
loss = history.history['loss']
val_loss = history.history['val_loss']
epochs = range(len(AUC))

plt.plot(epochs, AUC, 'g', label='Training AUC')
plt.plot(epochs, val_AUC, 'b', label='Validation AUC')
plt.title('Training and validation AUC')
plt.legend(loc=0)
plt.draw()
fig1 = plt.gcf()
fig1.savefig('AUC.png',dpi=100)
plt.show()

plt.plot(epochs, loss, 'g', label='Training loss')
plt.plot(epochs, val_loss, 'b', label='Validation loss')
plt.title('Training and validation loss')
plt.legend(loc=0)
plt.draw()
fig2 = plt.gcf()
fig2.savefig('loss.png',dpi=100)
plt.show()

# metrics

model_analysis = pd.DataFrame()
model_analysis["y"] = yvalid
model_analysis["y_hat"] = np.reshape((model.predict(xvalid) > 0.5).astype("int32"),xvalid.shape[0])
model_analysis["hit"] = model_analysis["y"] == model_analysis["y_hat"]
accs = sum(model_analysis["hit"])/len(model_analysis["hit"])
F1 = f1_score(model_analysis["y"],model_analysis["y_hat"])
tn, fp, fn, tp = confusion_matrix(model_analysis["y"], model_analysis["y_hat"]).ravel()
print("Classification accuracy on the validation set is " + str(accs*100) + "% correct predictions")
## Classification accuracy on the validation set is 94.87148762806302% correct predictions
print("F1 score on the validation set is " + str(F1))

Conclusions

Finally and as a mode of conclusion, attached to this report a csv file called “validation_prediction.csv” which contains the user_ids and a prediction in the form of 1 or 0 (churn or not respectively) for the test set, that is, all the users whose churn status was unclear in the data. Whether the predicted churn status of these users corresponds to the real one is the ultimate test for this model at present.

95% accuracy is a pretty good result, but surely it can be improved. Beyond many statistical or machine learning tools that could be used to improve the result further, more information on the users could potentially be beneficial as well. In fact almost any information may turn out to be predictive of churn; total transactions amount, number of transactions, type of customer are just a few variables that come to mind as likely to improve the model. In future research exercises it is recommended to:

  • Explore the causes behind the churn rate in Germany.

  • Seek to confirm or disprove the hypotheses regarding churn rates nearing December 2020

  • Explore further the interesting bimodal age churn peaks against the unimodal age no-churn peak

  • Explore the meaning of the skew in contact propensity for both groups of customers and its consequences for the potential re-engagement of those who churned.
